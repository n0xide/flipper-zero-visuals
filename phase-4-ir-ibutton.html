Phase 4 — IR + iButton

Two short-range protocols that are easy to study because they were never designed for security. Infrared is line-of-sight broadcast at 38 kHz; 1-Wire iButton is a single-line bus with fixed serial numbers. Both broken by design — that's the lesson.

Legality boundary — the lightest of any phase.
  • IR is unlicensed and unregulated almost everywhere. Transmitting at your own TV is legal; transmitting at a coworker's TV across the open office is just rude. The Flipper's IR power is harmless to non-IR systems.
  • IR jamming a movie theater / classroom projector is disruption, not unauthorized access — but it can still draw a misuse-of-equipment charge or a venue ban. Don't.
  • iButton dongles you own (your gym locker fob, blank DS1990A keys you bought) — full read/clone is legal. This is the primary lab workflow.
  • Cloning a building's iButton (apartment intercom, restricted-area access) without authorization is unauthorized access, same framing as the card phase. Owner consent or written authorization required.

1. IR physics — why everyone uses 38 kHz

Infrared remotes don't transmit a steady "high" — that would be lost in ambient IR (sunlight, fluorescents, your body heat). Instead they modulate a carrier at ~38 kHz, and the receiver uses a bandpass filter tuned to that exact frequency to reject everything else.

Why 38 kHz specifically: high enough to reject incandescent flicker (120 Hz) and fluorescent ballasts (a few kHz); low enough that cheap PIN-photodiode receivers can demodulate it without exotic optics. The standard TSOP4838 / VS1838B receivers have a bandpass tuned exactly here. Sony uses 40 kHz, Bang & Olufsen uses 455 kHz (rare); the Flipper auto-detects on receive but you set carrier on transmit.

2. IR protocols — NEC vs RC5 vs SIRC vs Pronto

Four distinct encoding schemes share the same 38 kHz carrier. Same LED, same receiver, different bit-timing rules. Click each tab to see the waveform side-by-side.

Side-by-side properties

ProtocolCarrierEncodingFrame structureBit rateUsed by
NEC 38 kHz Pulse distance (gap length = bit value) 9 ms lead-in + 4.5 ms space + 8-bit address + ~addr + 8-bit cmd + ~cmd + stop ~1.06 kbps Most TVs (LG, Samsung, Toshiba), set-top boxes, AC remotes
RC5 36 kHz Manchester (G.E. Thomas) — transitions = bit value Start (1) + field bit + toggle bit + 5-bit addr + 6-bit cmd = 14 bits ~700 bps Philips, some European brands. Toggle bit lets receivers distinguish "repeat" from "two presses".
SIRC 40 kHz Pulse width (long burst = 1, short burst = 0) 2.4 ms lead-in + 7-bit cmd + 5-bit addr (12-bit) or extended 15/20-bit ~1 kbps Sony — TVs, audio, BluRay; PS2 DVD remote and PS3 BD remote IR receiver (PS1 had no native IR support). Repeats every 45 ms while pressed.
Pronto Variable "Hex format" — raw burst/gap pairs in microseconds Header (carrier freq + repeat counts) + N burst-pair values — Philips Pronto universal remotes; the lingua franca for sharing custom codes online (RemoteCentral). Flipper imports Pronto format.
The decoder's job: sample the demodulated envelope at 1 MHz, measure burst/gap durations, match against the protocol's bit-timing table. The Flipper's IR app auto-classifies by lead-in shape — NEC's distinctive 9ms+4.5ms pulse pair is unmistakable. RC5 has no lead-in; SIRC's lead is 2.4 ms; Pronto comes in raw and isn't a "protocol" so much as a transport format.

3. What the Flipper can do with IR

IR has no security model at all. Whatever brand of remote sits in your hand can be cloned in a few seconds. The interesting question is what you can do without the original remote.

OperationFlipper capabilityHow
Clone an existing remote (button-by-button) trivial "Learn New Remote" → point original at Flipper → press each button → save. Works on every protocol above.
Use a remote you don't have (TV, AC, projector) supported "Universal Remote" → pick device class → brand DB cycles through known codes for that brand. Same idea as a Logitech Harmony.
Brute-force a TV power-off (any brand) slow but works "TV-B-Gone" mode (Mitch Altman, 2004) — cycles through ~250+ known "Power" codes across all brands. Takes ~30s; one will hit. Famously turns off TVs in airports / waiting rooms.
Decode a captured remote signal automatic Receive mode classifies NEC/RC5/SIRC/Pronto automatically and shows the bit pattern.
Replay AC remote settings partial AC remotes send LONG frames (60–200 bits, encoding full state: temp, fan, mode). Captures only on whole-frame; if you got fan-only setting, you're stuck on fan-only.
Bidirectional channel? no No standard consumer IR receiver. CEC over HDMI does exist but isn't IR — it's a wired sideband. Flipper IR is one-way TX.
"TV-B-Gone" use cases: taught in this curriculum as a demonstration of how trivially-unauthenticated IR is. Public deployment (airport TV, restaurant screens) is mild disruption — not unauthorized access — but venue owners and staff will be unhappy. Cool party trick, bad first impression with security personnel.

4. 1-Wire iButton — one pin, parasitic power, broadcast bus

The Dallas / Maxim DS1990A — a stainless-steel "button" the size of a watch battery — is the canonical 1-Wire device. Two contacts: a single signal wire and ground. Power, clock, and data all share that one wire.

The 64-bit ROM = 8-bit family code (0x01 for DS1990A) + 48-bit unique serial + 8-bit CRC. Burned at the factory; can never change. That's the whole "security model" — the receiver trusts whatever ROM the device reports. No challenge, no encryption, nothing. This was Dallas Semiconductor's 1992 design philosophy: cheap, single-wire, unforgeable against the threat model of 1992 (no consumer hardware could fake it). The threat model aged badly.

5. DS1990A read — what's actually on the wire

The whole exchange: master pulses a reset, slave answers with a presence pulse, master sends READ ROM command (0x33), slave clocks out its 64 ROM bits. Total: about 2 ms.

Phase-by-phase

PhaseMasterSlaveTiming
Reset pulse Pulls DATA low — ≥ 480 μs LOW
Presence pulse Releases DATA (pull-up takes it high) Detects rising edge, waits ~30 μs, pulls DATA low for ~120 μs Slave LOW for 60–240 μs after master release
READ ROM cmd Clocks out 0x33 (LSB first), 1 bit per 60 μs slot Listens 8 bits × 60 μs = 480 μs
ROM transmission Initiates each read slot by pulling DATA low ~1 μs If bit = 0: hold DATA low through slot. If bit = 1: release immediately (pull-up wins). 64 bits × 60 μs = 3.8 ms
CRC check Computes CRC-8 over the 56 data bits, compares to the received 8-bit CRC — (on-master, no wire activity)
Bit timing trick: the master always initiates every bit slot by pulling DATA low for 1–15 μs (the "read time slot start"). The slave decides what the bit value is by either holding LOW (→ 0) or letting the pull-up restore HIGH (→ 1). One wire, simultaneous direction control, no clock signal. Elegant — and exploited by every clone tool ever made, including the Flipper.

6. Clone workflow — read → write to blank

Identical pattern to the LF RFID workflow in Phase 3. The "security" rests on the assumption that nobody has a programmable DS1990A. They are $1 on AliExpress.

Step 1 — Read original

Press original to Flipper iButton contact. Flipper auto-detects family code, dumps ROM, validates CRC.

Step 2 — Write to blank (RW1990 / TM2004)

Press blank to Flipper contact. Flipper sends the proprietary "write enable" sequence (varies by chip) then clocks out the new ROM bits.

Compatible blank chips

Blank chipEmulatesNotes
RW1990 / RW1990.1 / RW1990.2DS1990AMost common. ~$1 each. Flipper writes directly.
TM2004DS1990A + DS1971 EEPROMUsed for some intercom systems. Flipper supports basic write.
Cyfral / MetakomRussian intercom standardsDifferent protocol entirely (not 1-Wire). Flipper supports separately.
DS1990A genuine Maxim—NOT writable. Factory-burned ROM. The original.
The defense: for any new install, switch to a challenge-response RFID system (DESFire on HF, see Phase 3) and treat iButton as legacy. For existing iButton deployments where rip-and-replace isn't feasible, add a second factor — usually keypad PIN + iButton. The fob alone can be cloned; the PIN is held by the person.
