Phase 2 — Sub-GHz Radio

Interactive visualizations for the deepest single domain in the Flipper curriculum: modulation, encoding, fixed vs rolling codes, RollJam mechanics, and brute-force math.

Legality boundary — read before pressing TX.
  • Receiving (passive) is generally legal on the ISM bands shown below. You may scan, decode, and analyze captured packets without permission.
  • Transmitting is regulated almost everywhere. In the US, the Flipper's TX is permitted on the unlicensed ISM bands at low power for short bursts (FCC Part 15), but jamming, replay against systems you don't own, or operating above licensed thresholds is a federal offense (Communications Act §333). Equivalent laws apply in the EU (CEPT/ETSI), UK (Wireless Telegraphy Act), and most other jurisdictions.
  • Replaying a captured signal at a target you do not own (a neighbor's garage, an unfamiliar car) is unauthorized access regardless of how easy the technique is.
Every technique below is taught fully — RollJam, KeeLoq cipher internals, brute force — because understanding them is the only way to defend against them. The line is who you point the antenna at: your own hardware, junk-yard remotes you bought, or a target under written authorization — yes. Anything else — no.

2. Modulation — ASK/OOK vs 2-FSK

Both diagrams below transmit the exact same bits. The difference is how those bits are pressed onto the carrier wave. Click bits to flip them.

Bit pattern:
8 bits, MSB-first

ASK / OOK — "carrier on or off"

A 1 turns the carrier on; a 0 turns it off. Cheapest possible scheme. Most $5 433 MHz remotes use this. Vulnerable to amplitude noise.

2-FSK — "two carriers, switch between them"

A 1 is a slightly higher carrier frequency; a 0 is a slightly lower one. Robust against amplitude noise. Used by Z-Wave, KeeLoq FSK variants, Hörmann.

Watch this: in OOK the wave amplitude is the signal — flat means 0. In FSK the wave is always there but its frequency carries the bit. A noise spike that confuses OOK barely affects FSK.

3. Encoding — NRZ vs Manchester vs PWM

Modulation is how the carrier carries bits. Encoding is how bits are laid out as a waveform shape. The two are orthogonal — you can have OOK with any of these encodings. Same input bits below produce three totally different waveforms.

Data bits:

NRZ — Non-Return-to-Zero

A 1 is high; a 0 is low. Problem: a run of identical bits looks like a flat line — receiver loses clock sync. DC bias on long runs.

Manchester

Every bit is a transition: a 1 is high-then-low; a 0 is low-then-high (G.E. Thomas convention — the one almost every RF remote uses). Self-clocking — there's always a transition mid-bit. Used by Princeton/PT2240 and most fixed-code remotes. Note: IEEE 802.3 Ethernet defines the opposite mapping; both conventions exist in the wild, always check the datasheet.

PWM — Pulse Width Modulation

A 1 is a long pulse + short gap; a 0 is a short pulse + long gap. The "TE" (timing element) is the base unit. Common in OOK remotes.

Pitfall: "OOK and Manchester are the same" — they're not. OOK is the modulation (how the carrier is keyed). Manchester is the encoding (how bits become a waveform shape). A remote can be OOK + NRZ or OOK + Manchester. Different waveforms on the air.

4. Fixed-code vs rolling-code remote

Press the same button twice. Compare the captures. If identical → fixed-code, trivially clonable. If different → rolling-code, cryptographically protected.

Each press appends one line.

BROKEN Fixed-code (PT2262 / Princeton)

24-bit address+data. Same bits every press.

Capture once → replay forever. The address is hardcoded by DIP switches inside the remote.

SAFE Rolling-code (KeeLoq HCS301)

66 bits: 32-bit cipher (counter + button + overflow + 10-bit discrimination) + 28-bit serial + 4-bit button + 2-bit status. The button is sent twice — once encrypted, once plaintext — so the receiver can route the press before decrypting. Cipher block changes every press.

Counter increments each press → totally different ciphertext. Receiver tracks last counter, rejects replays.

Lab 2.3 takeaway: the two-press comparison is the cheapest possible diagnostic. If you can't tell which class you're looking at, you can't reason about whether replay attacks will work.

5. KeeLoq packet anatomy — what's actually transmitted

The 66 bits on the air every time you press a rolling-code button. Hover a section to see what it is.

Why replay fails: the ciphertext is bound to the counter, which only goes up. The receiver tracks the highest counter it has ever accepted and rejects anything ≤ that. A captured packet, replayed, is rejected before its plaintext is even unpacked.
Side note — KeeLoq's historical break (Eisenbarth et al., 2008)

Academic teams extracted the manufacturer master key from a single receiver via differential power analysis (a side-channel attack on the receiver, not the cipher itself). With the master key + a serial number from a captured packet, you can derive any remote's 64-bit secret in that brand's ecosystem and clone arbitrarily. Patched in modern variants by using per-deployment masters rather than a globally-shared master.

6. RollJam — the only practical attack on naive rolling codes

Samy Kamkar's 2015 attack against rolling-code remotes that lack timestamps. Step through the sequence below.

Step 0 / 7
Step 0: Initial state. Remote counter at 42 (next press will transmit). Receiver has accepted up through counter 41. Attacker has nothing.
Defense against RollJam: (1) two-way protocols — the receiver responds, the remote knows whether the press took effect; (2) tight resync windows — drop a counter older than N seconds; (3) timestamps in the payload — packet expires after a few seconds. Modern Chamberlain Security+ 2.0, Hörmann BiSecur, and most post-2018 systems use one or more of these.

The realistic attack on fixed-code remotes is not brute force — it's pattern targeting. The Sub-GHz Bruteforcer FAP iterates known DIP-switch patterns, common factory-default addresses, and the small subset of codes a given installer batch was likely to use. That cuts 24-bit keyspaces from "weeks" to "minutes" for plausible cases. Generic brute force remains useless.
