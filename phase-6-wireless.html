Phase 6 — Wireless Extensions (BLE / WiFi / NRF24)

Three different wireless domains, each requiring different hardware. The legal landscape is sharper here than earlier phases — read Phase 0 again before any of these labs.

Legality boundary — wireless is where the laws bite hardest.
  • Passive WiFi capture in airodump / monitor mode — legal in the US (open-air RF is fair game to receive), but some EU jurisdictions treat the contents of unencrypted traffic as protected once it's identifiable to an account. Capturing a handshake near a café is a grey zone; cracking it offline against your own AP is unambiguously fine.
  • Deauth attacks (§3 below) — practical demonstration of broken WPA2 management frames. In the US, intentional disruption of a WiFi network you don't own is illegal interference (FCC enforcement actions: hotels fined $600k+ for deauth-jamming guest hotspots, 2014–2016). Practice in a Faraday box or against a dedicated lab AP — never in production airspace.
  • Evil-twin / rogue AP (§4) — standing up a rogue AP that captures credentials from clients that join is unauthorized access in nearly every jurisdiction, including the US (Wiretap Act + CFAA). Permitted only on isolated lab networks or with explicit red-team authorization that names the SSID being impersonated.
  • BLE passive observation — generally legal. Active probing/connecting to devices you don't own is not.
  • NRF24 / MouseJack injection — keystroke injection into someone else's keyboard receiver is unauthorized access. Vendor disclosure (Bastille's 2016 publication) is the legal framing for studying the technique; deployment requires authorization.
Every attack below is taught in full — defending against deauth, evil twin, MouseJack, or PSK cracking requires understanding exactly how they work. The technique is knowledge; the deployment is the regulated act.

2. WiFi WPA2 4-way handshake — what you capture and crack

Every time a client joins a WPA2 network, four management frames are exchanged. They contain enough material to crack the PSK offline if you have a wordlist that contains it.

Step 0 / 6
Step 0: Initial state. AP has the PMK derived from the network's PSK + SSID. Client knows the PSK (the passphrase the user typed). They've never talked. Attacker is sitting nearby with a card in monitor mode.
The crack: after capturing all 4 frames, the attacker has Anonce, Snonce, both MAC addresses, and the MIC. Run hashcat -m 22000 capture.hccapx wordlist.txt — for each candidate passphrase, derive the PMK, compute the PTK, recompute MIC. Match = PSK found. Speed depends on PSK strength (see §6).

Shortcut: PMKID attack (Steube, 2018) — no client, no handshake

Many WPA/WPA2 APs include the PMKID in the very first EAPOL-Key frame of an association attempt — or even in the unsolicited M1 sent after an associate request. One round-trip from the attacker, no handshake to wait for, no client needed.

Other shortcuts worth knowing:
  • KRACK (Vanhoef, 2017) — nonce-reuse against the 4-way handshake itself. Lets an attacker force PTK reinstallation and replay/decrypt traffic. Mitigated by client/AP patches, but legacy gear in the wild is still vulnerable.
  • WPA3 SAE replaces the PSK exchange with Dragonfly (a PAKE) — captures become useless because there's no offline-crackable handshake. Dragonblood (2019) found side-channel and downgrade weaknesses; mostly patched by 2021.
  • OWE (Opportunistic Wireless Encryption) — replaces "open" guest networks with an unauthenticated Diffie-Hellman exchange; you still don't trust the AP, but a passive sniffer can no longer read the traffic.

3. Why deauth still works — PMF on vs off

Protected Management Frames (PMF) was added in 802.11w (2009) to fix the gaping hole that management frames had no authentication. WPA3 mandates PMF. WPA2 makes it optional. Most home networks: optional, often off.

PMF disabled (WPA2 default on many networks)

Why this works: deauth/disassoc frames pre-PMF are unauthenticated. Any device can spoof the AP's MAC and send "you are deauthenticated" to any client. Client has no way to verify it came from the real AP.

PMF enabled (WPA3 mandatory, WPA2 opt-in)

PMF adds a MIC (Message Integrity Code) computed with the session keys to management frames. Attacker doesn't have the session keys, so spoofed deauths fail validation. Recommendation in any audit: enable PMF, prefer WPA3.

4. Evil twin / rogue AP attack

Stand up an AP with the same SSID as a target network. Clients with that SSID in their preferred network list will (often) auto-connect to whichever has stronger signal.

What this gets the attacker: any traffic the client sends post-association. Captive portal phishing for creds. DNS spoofing for follow-on. SSL stripping where TLS isn't enforced. Note: modern browsers / HSTS-preloaded sites mitigate the SSL strip path.
Defense: WPA2-Enterprise with EAP-TLS (client validates RADIUS server's certificate — defeats evil twin). For PSK networks: PMF + client OS that flags "weaker security" mismatches. User training: "if the network shows as open after you joined a WPA one, that's not your network."

5. MouseJack — wireless keystroke injection at 30 m

Marc Newlin's 2016 attack class. Many cheap 2.4 GHz mice/keyboards use the NRF24 chip with unencrypted, unauthenticated keyboard payloads. A nearby attacker injects keystrokes into the USB receiver from across the office.

6. WPA2 PSK crack-time — why 12 random characters matter

Once you have a captured handshake, time-to-crack depends entirely on PSK entropy. hashcat -m 22000 on a mid-range GPU sits around 0.5 Mh/s; an RTX 4090 pushes ~2 Mh/s; a rented cloud rig (8× A100) climbs past 10 Mh/s. Drag the slider to model your threat.
