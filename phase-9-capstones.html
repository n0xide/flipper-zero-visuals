Phase 9 — Capstone scenarios

Each capstone is a real-shape engagement — a target with multiple attack surfaces from Phases 2–7 simultaneously visible. Pick a scenario, walk the site map, point to surfaces, justify each as in-scope or out-of-scope. Then write the audit report from Phase 8\'s checklist. Five canonical environments: home, office, vehicle, consumer IoT, and operational technology.

Legality boundary — capstones are bigger engagements, with sharper authorization needs.
  • Your own home / your own car / your own IoT device: full scope; document and treat as a personal training engagement.
  • Friend or family\'s home / vehicle: requires their written permission, naming each surface in scope. Verbal "sure, hack my router" is not sufficient — write it down.
  • Office / corporate environment: needs the legal entity\'s authorization, not just an employee\'s. Statement of work, scope, time window, rollback plan, named SOC contact.
  • Vehicle dealership / manufacturer: almost certainly out of scope. Bug bounty programs (Tesla, GM, Ford) define what is allowed and how to disclose.
  • OT / ICS / SCADA: the strictest tier. Active testing in production is usually never authorized. Lab replicas (with vendor permission), bug bounty programs, or read-only passive observation are the legitimate paths.
Every surface below is taught fully because defenders need to see what an attacker sees. Active testing only after written authorization that names the surface.

    2. Engagement playbook — same shape for every capstone

    Whatever the scenario, the same five-phase flow applies. The findings change; the structure is stable.

    PhaseWhat you doDeliverable / artifact
    1. Pre-engagement Sign Statement of Work; agree scope, time window, contact tree, rollback plan. Identify out-of-scope surfaces explicitly. Signed SoW + scope document.
    2. Passive recon Sight survey; passive RF observation (no TX); photograph relevant surfaces. Identify what tech is deployed (badge format, AP model, lock brand). Site map (this page is a template); inventory of identified surfaces.
    3. Active testing Per the agreed scope: capture handshakes, attempt card reads with consent, attempt UART connection, etc. Stop and report immediately on anything outside scope. Test logs, evidence captures, findings register.
    4. Analysis Take captured material to a lab environment. Run offline crack / firmware analysis. Confirm vulnerabilities. Re-rank severity against the threat model. Validated findings list; CVSS scoring.
    5. Report Write the Phase 8 deliverable checklist sections that apply. Brief the client. Hand over evidence under encrypted transport. Schedule a re-test for any remediations. Final report; remediation owner table; re-test plan.
    The pattern that survives every engagement: capture passively first, escalate to active only when the scope makes it explicit, and write the defense recommendations as you go. The capstone\'s purpose is to integrate Phases 2-7 into one engagement; the deliverable is the Phase 8 audit report.
