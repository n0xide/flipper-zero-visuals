Phase 7 — GPIO + Hardware Hacking

Where the Flipper stops being a contactless multi-tool and starts being a hardware bench in your pocket. Visualizations for the GPIO header, three bus protocols (UART/SPI/I²C), SOIC-8 flash dumping, the audit chain, and ARM RDP levels.

Legality boundary — hardware hacking pivots on ownership.
  • A device you bought, opened, and own — UART boot logs, SPI flash dumps, SWD halt-and-debug, even chip-off. All legal. Right-to-repair laws (US DMCA §1201 exemptions, EU Right-to-Repair Directive) explicitly protect security research and reverse-engineering of devices you own.
  • A junk-yard router, an end-of-life smart bulb, an eBay teardown unit — same. The signal is: you have the right to disassemble and probe what you own.
  • A device under maintenance contract / warranty — opening voids warranty but is still legal. Tampering with a leased device (Tesla, John Deere, medical device) gets messier and may be barred by contract; not criminal in most jurisdictions but contractually defensible.
  • Someone else's device, even temporarily — not yours. Probing a coworker's IoT thermostat, a hotel TV, or a borrowed router without owner consent is unauthorized access in nearly every jurisdiction.
  • Vendor disclosure — vulnerabilities discovered in a device you own and disclosed to the vendor under a reasonable embargo is protected research speech in the US and most of Europe. Doing the same work and weaponizing it against deployed third-party devices is not.
Every technique below assumes the target is your hardware (or your authorized test bench). The defenses in §5 — RDP, secure boot, glitch protection — are the deliverables a client gets when you finish the audit.

Power-rail safety: the Flipper drives at 3.3V logic. Connecting Flipper's 3.3V rail to a target that's already self-powered is the #1 way to fry one or both devices. Always common-ground first; never share power rails unless intentional. 5V targets need a level shifter on lines the Flipper drives.

How to identify which protocol you're looking at on a PCB: count pins to a chip, look up its datasheet by part number. Most flash chips are SPI (8-pin SOIC). Most config EEPROMs are I²C (8-pin SOIC). Debug headers (3-4 pins, often labeled) are UART. SWD is a 2-pin (+power+gnd) header, sometimes broken out as standardized 10-pin or 20-pin connectors.

5. ARM Cortex-M Read-Out Protection (RDP) levels

STM32 family example. Levels 0/1/2 trade convenience for security; level 2 is irreversible. Same idea on other ARM vendors (NXP CRP, Nordic APPROTECT, etc.).

CapabilityRDP Level 0 (off)RDP Level 1RDP Level 2 (irreversible)
Read flash via SWD/JTAG ✓ wide open ✗ blocked ✗ blocked
Read RAM via SWD/JTAG ✓ wide open ✓ readable ✗ blocked
Reflash via SWD/JTAG ✓ allowed ✓ but erases flash first ✗ blocked
Halt + step CPU ✓ debugger attaches ✓ debugger attaches ✗ debugger refused
Downgrade to lower level — ✓ via mass-erase ✗ NEVER (option byte fused)
What an attacker can do Full dump in seconds Erase + reflash own firmware (but original lost) Glitch attack or physical decap only

Reality check — what you'll see

  • Hobby / consumer dev boards: almost always RDP 0. Trivial dump.
  • Cheap IoT products: often RDP 0 (cost-cutting, dev convenience).
  • Mid-tier consumer: usually RDP 1. Original firmware safe; chip is bricked from your perspective.
  • Payment terminals, medical, automotive: RDP 2 + secure elements + glitch detection.

When SWD is locked: glitching

RDP-level checks happen at specific clock cycles. A precisely-timed voltage glitch (drop Vcc for ~50 ns) during that check can cause the CPU to misread the option byte and skip protection. ChipWhisperer is the canonical hobbyist platform. Multi-week learning curve, but it's the door that opens when SWD won't.
